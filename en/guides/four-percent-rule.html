The 4% Rule Explained: Safe Withdrawal Rate for Early Retirement

The 4% rule is the cornerstone of the FIRE movement. It says you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one, adjust that amount for inflation each year, and have a very high probability of never running out of money over a 30+ year retirement. Here's how it works, where it came from, and when it might not apply.

Origins: The Trinity Study

In 1998, three professors at Trinity University published a study examining withdrawal rates from stock/bond portfolios over every 30-year period from 1926-1995. Their finding: a 4% initial withdrawal rate, adjusted annually for inflation, had a 95%+ success rate for portfolios with 50-75% stocks.

Annual Withdrawal = Portfolio Value × 0.04

How to Apply It

Example: If your FIRE number is $1,000,000, you withdraw $40,000 in year one. If inflation is 3%, you withdraw $41,200 in year two. The portfolio continues growing at ~7%, so your 4% withdrawal is sustainable indefinitely.

When the 4% Rule Might Fail

  • Very early retirement (50+ years): Consider 3.25-3.5% for 50-year horizons
  • High market valuations at retirement: Starting during a market peak increases sequence-of-returns risk
  • Inflexible spending: If you can't cut expenses during down markets, use 3.5%
