How Compound Interest Works — With Real Examples

Compound interest is why investing early matters more than investing a lot. It's interest earned on interest — and over decades, the effect is staggering. Here's how it works, with examples you can verify yourself using our free calculator.

The Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) + PMT × ((1 + r/n)^(nt) - 1) / (r/n)

Where P = starting principal, r = annual rate, n = compounding periods/year, t = years, PMT = regular contribution

Example: The $10,000 Seed

Invest $10,000 at age 25 at 7% annual return, never add another dollar: At 65, it's worth $149,745. That's nearly 15× the original investment — all from compounding alone.

The Rule of 72

Years to Double = 72 ÷ Annual Return (%)

At 7%, money doubles every ~10.3 years. At 10%, every 7.2 years. This quick mental math helps you estimate growth without a calculator.

Compounding Works Both Ways

Just as compounding builds wealth, it also accelerates debt. A $5,000 credit card balance at 22% APR doubles in ~3.3 years if unpaid. This is why aggressive debt payoff is mathematically equivalent to earning that APR as an investment return.
