How to Calculate Your FIRE Number (Step by Step)

The FIRE number is the amount of money you need invested to achieve financial independence and never work again. It's the foundation of the FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) movement. Here's exactly how to calculate yours — plus a free calculator to do it in seconds.

The Core Formula: The 4% Rule

The most widely used method comes from the Trinity Study, which found that withdrawing 4% of your portfolio annually, adjusted for inflation, has a very high probability of lasting 30+ years.

FIRE Number = Annual Expenses × 25

Why 25? Because 1 ÷ 0.04 = 25. If you spend $40,000/year, your FIRE number is $40,000 × 25 = $1,000,000.

Step-by-Step Calculation

Step 1: Calculate Your Annual Expenses

Track every dollar you spend for 3-6 months. Include rent/mortgage, food, utilities, insurance, transportation, entertainment. Don't forget irregular expenses like car repairs or medical bills — average them out monthly.

Example: Your monthly spending is $3,500. Annual expenses = $3,500 × 12 = $42,000.

Step 2: Apply the 4% Rule

Multiply your annual expenses by 25.

Example: $42,000 × 25 = $1,050,000. This is your baseline FIRE number — the minimum you need invested.

Step 3: Adjust for Inflation

If you're 10 years away from FIRE, your expenses will grow with inflation. At 3% inflation, $42,000 becomes ~$56,500 in 10 years. Your inflation-adjusted FIRE number would be ~$1,412,500.

Step 4: Factor in Your Savings Rate

Your savings rate determines how fast you reach FIRE:

  • Save 20% of income → ~37 years to FIRE
  • Save 50% of income → ~17 years to FIRE
  • Save 70% of income → ~8.5 years to FIRE

Beyond the Basics: Coast FIRE

Coast FIRE is the point where your existing investments will grow to your FIRE number on their own — even if you never contribute another dollar. You just need to cover your living expenses until retirement age. Our free FIRE calculator calculates your Coast FIRE age automatically.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting taxes: The 4% rule assumes your withdrawals are your total income. Budget for capital gains tax.
  • Ignoring healthcare: Health insurance costs can be $500-$1,500/month in the US before Medicare.
  • Using optimistic returns: The 4% rule assumes ~7% annual returns. Don't plan on 10%+.
  • Not accounting for lifestyle inflation: Your FIRE expenses should be realistic, not aspirational.

Bottom line: Your FIRE number is your annual expenses × 25. Use a calculator to run different scenarios with inflation, contributions, and Coast FIRE. The sooner you know your number, the sooner you can plan to reach it.
