How Long to Pay Off Debt with Extra Payments

Adding even small extra payments to your debt can slash years off your payoff timeline and save thousands in interest. Here's exactly how to calculate your debt-free date — and a free tool to do it instantly.

Why Extra Payments Are So Powerful

Every extra dollar you pay goes directly to principal. That reduces the balance on which future interest is calculated — a compounding effect in reverse. Consider this example:

$10,000 credit card at 22% APR, $300 minimum payment: Paying only the minimum takes 47 months and costs $3,900 in interest. Adding just $100/month extra slashes it to 30 months and saves $1,400.

Snowball vs Avalanche: Which Gets You Debt-Free Faster?

Avalanche (highest APR first) mathematically minimizes total interest and time. But snowball (smallest balance first) provides psychological wins that help you stick with it. Research shows people using snowball are more likely to actually finish.

The best approach: calculate both and see the actual difference. If avalanche saves you $200 in interest but takes 6 months longer to see your first debt cleared, snowball might be the better choice.

How to Calculate Your Debt-Free Date

  1. List every debt with balance, APR, and minimum payment
  2. Decide your extra monthly budget (even $50 helps)
  3. Pick a strategy — snowball or avalanche
  4. Calculate month by month: interest accrues → you pay minimum + extra toward target debt → repeat until all debts hit $0
